How to choose an induction cooktop

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

An induction cooktop uses electromagnetic energy to heat your cookware directly, offering faster boiling, precise temperature control, and a cool-to-the-touch surface. Your budget, kitchen setup, and preferred features, like number of burners, power levels, and safety options, will guide your choice, but any induction model will outperform gas or electric in efficiency and responsiveness.

Is induction cooking right for your kitchen?

Induction cooktops are wildly efficient and safe because the heat is generated inside the pan rather than on the surface. The cooktop stays cool to the touch, which means less risk of burns and easier cleanup. But there is a catch: you need magnetic cookware. If your pots are aluminum, copper, or pure stainless steel (without a magnetic base), they won’t work. A quick test: see if a refrigerator magnet sticks to the bottom of your pan. Also think about your existing wiring. Most induction cooktops need a dedicated circuit, often 240 volts and 40 or 50 amps. Portable induction burners can plug into a standard 120-volt outlet, but a full-size built-in usually requires a professional installation. If you’re currently using gas, you may need to run a new electrical line and cap the gas line.

How many burners and what size do you need?

The number of burners (or “cooking zones”) ranges from two to six, with four being the most common for a household. Two-burner units are great for small apartments, dorm rooms, or as a supplement to a larger range. Four-burner models cover most home cooking needs. If you frequently cook large meals with multiple pots, think holiday dinners or meal prep, look for a five- or six-burner cooktop. Size matters for the cutout in your countertop. Standard widths are 30 inches (most common in North America) or 36 inches for more spacious kitchens. Some European brands offer 24-inch or even 60-centimeter models. Always measure your cutout dimensions before shopping; many induction cooktops have specific clearance requirements for ventilation and electrical conduit.

What power levels and features matter most?

Look for a cooktop that offers a wide range of power levels, typically from a gentle simmer (around 1 or 2) to a high-power boost for rapid boiling. A good induction cooktop will have at least 10 power levels and often a dedicated “power boost” that delivers maximum wattage to one burner for a short time. This is fantastic for quickly bringing water to a boil or searing meat. Other useful features include a timer (so you can set a burner to turn off automatically), a child lock to prevent accidental activation, and a bridge function that links two zones into one long cooking area for griddles or rectangular pans. Some premium models have a “flex zone” that lets you place a large pot across two burners and have the cooktop automatically detect the pan’s size and shape.

How does installation and ventilation affect your choice?

Induction cooktops don’t produce combustion fumes, so you can use a lower-CFM (cubic feet per minute) range hood compared to what you’d need for gas. But you still need to exhaust steam, smoke, and odors. A ducted hood is best, but a recirculating hood can work if ducting isn’t possible. As mentioned, electrical requirements are critical. Most full-size induction cooktops require a 240-volt, 40- or 50-amp dedicated circuit. If you’re replacing an old electric coil or smooth-top cooktop, that circuit may already be in place. If you’re switching from gas, budget for an electrician to run new wiring. Portable induction burners, on the other hand, plug into a standard 120-volt outlet but will draw about 15 amps, so avoid using them on the same circuit as other high-power appliances.

Which brands are known for reliable induction cooktops?

Several brands have strong reputations in the induction market. GE Profile and Cafe models are praised for their robust build and intuitive controls. Frigidaire Gallery offers a great mix of features at a more approachable price point. On the premium side, Bosch and Thermador are known for smooth operation and very even heat distribution, though they command a higher budget. Samsung and LG also produce induction cooktops with smart features like Wi-Fi connectivity and voice control, which some people find handy and others view as unnecessary complexity. When reading reviews, pay attention to feedback about knob longevity (if the model has physical knobs) and touch-control responsiveness. Also look for complaints about noise: some induction cooktops produce a high-pitched hum or fan noise. A well-reviewed model from a reputable brand will usually handle these issues well.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need special pots and pans for induction?

Yes. Induction requires cookware with a magnetic bottom. Cast iron, enameled cast iron, and most stainless steel with a magnetic base work well. If a magnet sticks to the pan, it’s induction-ready. If it doesn’t, you’ll need new cookware.

Is induction cooking safer than gas or electric?

Generally, yes. The cooktop surface stays relatively cool because the heat is in the pan, so burn risks are lower. Induction also turns off automatically when no pan is detected, and there’s no open flame or hot coils. That said, the pan itself gets very hot, so handles can burn you if touched.

Can I use a portable induction burner on a standard outlet?

Yes, most portable induction burners plug into a standard 120-volt household outlet and work fine for single-pot cooking. However, they can draw up to 15 amps, so it’s best to avoid using other high-wattage appliances on the same circuit at the same time.

Does induction cooking affect cookware longevity?

Not usually, but very thin pans may warp under the intense rapid heat of a power-boost function. Thick, high-quality cookware handles induction well. Also, some older enameled pans with thin steel layers can develop hot spots. Stick to mid-range or premium cookware for best results.

Will my induction cooktop work during a power outage?

No. Induction cooktops require electricity. If you lose power, you cannot cook on them. If this is a concern, keep a backup camp stove or a portable propane burner handy.

How do I clean an induction cooktop?

Because the surface stays cool, spills don’t bake on. Wipe up messes with a soft cloth and a mild cooktop cleaner after the unit is turned off but while the glass is still warm (not hot). Avoid abrasive pads, which can scratch the ceramic glass. For stubborn stains, a specialized induction cooktop scraper works well.

In shortChoosing the right induction cooktop comes down to matching your kitchen’s electrical setup, your cookware collection, and your cooking style. Focus on burner count, power range, and safety features that matter to you. A bit of upfront planning, checking your circuit and testing your pans, will save you headaches later. With the right model, you’ll enjoy fast, precise, and easy-to-clean cooking for years.
